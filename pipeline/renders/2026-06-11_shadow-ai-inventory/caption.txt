Most AI risk in companies right now is not a sci-fi attack. It is shadow AI, the tools your team adopted before anyone reviewed them.

Microsoft's 2024 Work Trend Index found that 78 percent of people using AI at work bring their own tools, often unsanctioned. IBM's 2025 Cost of a Data Breach report found that one in five breached organizations traced a breach to shadow AI, that it added about $670,000 to the average breach cost, and that most breached organizations still had no AI governance in place. OWASP lists sensitive information disclosure as a top risk for LLM apps.

The problem is visibility, not malice. When a tool is not sanctioned, there is usually no logging, no retention policy, and no data-handling review behind it, so data flows out through paths security cannot see. The team often finds out only after the data has already left.

Defender move: start with visibility, not bans. Inventory which AI tools are actually in use, using your proxy, SSO, and browser telemetry. Offer a sanctioned alternative with clear data rules so people do not route around you. Then run every AI tool through the same vendor and security review as any other software.

You cannot govern the AI you cannot see. Inventory first, then write the policy.

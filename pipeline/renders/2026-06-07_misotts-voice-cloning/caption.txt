Miso Labs released MisoTTS, an 8 billion parameter emotive text-to-speech model with open weights. It can clone a voice, and the emotion behind it, from seconds of reference audio. The license is a modified MIT, so it is broadly usable, and the model watermarks its output by default.

The security angle is not a brand-new threat, it is a cheaper and more convincing one. Open, emotive voice cloning keeps eroding the oldest trust signal we have, which is recognizing someone's voice on the phone. That signal is what vishing, fake-relative scams, and CEO wire-transfer fraud run on. The FBI has warned for a while that criminals use AI-generated voice to impersonate relatives in crisis and to impersonate officials. A default watermark helps, but a motivated abuser can strip it, so do not lean on it.

The fix is process, not detection. Treat a familiar voice as no proof of identity. Verify any sensitive or financial request out-of-band: hang up and call back a known number, or use a code word you agreed on with family and your team. For wire transfers and payment approvals, require dual authorization, not a voice on the line.

A voice you recognize is not identity anymore. Verify the request on a channel the caller cannot fake.

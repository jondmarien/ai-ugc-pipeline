Miso Labs released Miso One, an 8 billion parameter open-weights voice model, also known as MisoTTS. It is an open alternative to closed services like ElevenLabs, built to sound warm and conversational rather than robotic, and it clones a voice and its emotion from a few seconds of audio. The license is a modified MIT, so it is broadly usable.

Two things make this matter for defenders. Miso Labs reports it responds in around 110 milliseconds, fast enough for real-time voice agents, so a cloned voice is no longer just a recording, it can hold a back-and-forth call. And the weights are on Hugging Face, so it runs offline on a laptop with no provider watching, moderating, or logging. It watermarks its output by default, but a motivated abuser can strip that.

None of this is a brand-new threat. It is a cheaper, faster, and more convincing version of one we already have. Open, emotive voice cloning keeps eroding the oldest trust signal we use, which is recognizing someone's voice on the phone. That signal is what vishing, fake-relative scams, and CEO wire-transfer fraud run on, and the FBI has warned for a while that criminals use AI-generated voice to impersonate relatives in crisis and to impersonate officials.

The fix is process, not detection. Treat a familiar voice as no proof of identity. Verify any sensitive or financial request out-of-band: hang up and call back a known number, or use a code word you agreed on with family and your team. For wire transfers and payment approvals, require dual authorization, not a voice on the line.

A voice you recognize is not identity anymore. Verify the request on a channel the caller cannot fake.

## Executive Summary

Jon, the format in the screenshots is not complicated because of the design. It is complicated because it combines **fast trend detection**, **aggressive hooks**, **cinematic AI visuals**, **simple carousel storytelling**, and **repeatable publishing discipline**. The weak point to watch is credibility. AI and cybersecurity content can easily drift into exaggerated claims, fake demos, or fear-based slop. If you want this to build authority rather than just impressions, your edge should be: **same viral packaging, better technical accuracy**.

The core workflow is straightforward. You find a timely AI/cybersecurity story, reduce it to one strong claim, build a 6–10 slide carousel around the claim, generate or source dramatic visuals, apply a consistent branded template, write a caption that expands the story, and post with a repeatable CTA. Instagram carousels work best when every slide uses the same dimensions, and current guidance commonly recommends **1080 × 1350 px portrait** because it occupies more feed space than square posts.[1] Buffer’s 2026 guide also notes that Instagram feed posts can use square, portrait, or landscape formats, while profile grid previews display in a **3:4 ratio**, so important text and faces should remain centered.[2]

> Overvisual’s Instagram carousel guide states: “All slides must use the same aspect ratio. Instagram crops mismatched slides to match the first image, which can cut off important content.”[1]

## 1. What This Content Style Actually Is

This style is best described as **AI-native tech tabloid meets educational carousel**. It is not a polished corporate infographic. It is not a deep technical whitepaper. It is a highly compressed content format that makes the viewer feel they are seeing a major development before everyone else.

The visual pattern is very consistent. Each cover uses a dramatic image, a large all-caps headline, a dark lower area for readability, and a small “swipe for more” prompt. The caption then translates the cover into a short narrative: what happened, why it matters, what it suggests about AI’s trajectory, and a simple question to drive comments.

| Element | What it does | How to adapt it for AI cybersecurity |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cover hook | Stops the scroll with a surprising claim | “A phishing kit now writes better emails than most interns” |
| Cinematic visual | Creates emotional context before the viewer reads | Hacker workstation, SOC room, glowing model interface, breached laptop |
| Short carousel | Turns one idea into a swipeable sequence | Threat, demo, attack path, defense, takeaway |
| Caption | Adds context and asks for engagement | Explain the issue without overclaiming |
| Repetition | Builds recognizable feed identity | Same fonts, spacing, color palette, and CTA every post |

The better version for you would lean less on vague “AI is getting scary” framing and more on **operator-level cybersecurity relevance**. That means your posts should answer one of four questions: **what changed, who is exposed, how the attack works, and what defenders should do next**.

## 2. The Best Niche Angle: AI in Cybersecurity

The obvious mistake would be covering “AI news” broadly. That puts you in direct competition with huge AI content pages. Your wedge should be narrower: **AI security, offensive AI, defensive AI, privacy failures, model abuse, cyber automation, agentic SOC workflows, AI governance, and real-world attack stories**.

A strong positioning line would be:

> **AI cybersecurity explained through viral carousels: real threats, real tools, no fake panic.**

This gives you room to use the same dramatic aesthetic while building credibility. The account should feel like a cybersecurity analyst explaining what the AI hype actually means for attackers, defenders, developers, and executives.

| Content pillar | Example post angle | Why it works |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Offensive AI | “A beginner used AI to build a working phishing flow in one night” | Shows how AI lowers attacker skill barriers |
| Defensive AI | “This SOC workflow cuts alert triage from 30 minutes to 3” | Makes AI useful rather than just scary |
| Model security | “Prompt injection just became a supply-chain problem” | Connects AI to classic security concepts |
| Privacy and data leakage | “Your chatbot logs may be the next breach report” | Relevant to companies adopting AI fast |
| Tool breakdowns | “5 AI security tools worth testing before your next audit” | Practical and saveable |
| Myth-busting | “No, AI did not hack the Pentagon by itself” | Differentiates you from low-trust viral pages |

## 3. A Repeatable Carousel Structure

A carousel should not be a random set of facts. It should have a small narrative arc. The best structure for AI cybersecurity is **hook, context, mechanism, implication, defense, takeaway**.

For Instagram, build most posts as 6–9 slides. Although Instagram supports up to 20 carousel slides according to Overvisual’s 2026 guide, shorter carousels are easier to finish and less likely to dilute the hook.[1] Use longer posts only when you are doing “10 examples,” “12 tools,” or a teardown.

| Slide | Purpose | Example copy |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Pattern interrupt | “AI phishing just crossed a line most teams are not ready for” |
| 2 | What happened | “A new wave of tools can generate targeted emails from public LinkedIn data.” |
| 3 | Why it matters | “The bottleneck used to be writing believable pretexts. AI removes that bottleneck.” |
| 4 | How it works | “The attacker feeds role, company, recent posts, and target objective into the model.” |
| 5 | Where teams fail | “Most awareness training still assumes generic phishing templates.” |
| 6 | Defensive move | “Train employees on context-aware social engineering, not just bad grammar.” |
| 7 | Tool/process | “Add mailbox anomaly detection and tighten OAuth app approvals.” |
| 8 | Takeaway | “AI did not invent phishing. It industrialized personalization.” |
| 9 | CTA | “Would your team catch this? Follow for AI security breakdowns.” |

## 4. The Cover Formula

The cover does most of the work. If the cover is weak, the caption and inner slides do not matter because people will never open it. The cover formula should be:

> **[Specific actor or object] + [unexpected AI action] + [clear consequence]**

Bad cover: “AI is changing cybersecurity.”  
Better cover: “An AI agent found exposed admin panels faster than a junior pentester.”  
Best cover: “A $20 AI workflow found 37 exposed admin panels before the security team did.”

Do not use fake precision unless you can support it. Numbers are powerful, but in cybersecurity they can make you look careless if they are invented. If you do not have verified numbers, use qualitative but concrete wording.

| Hook pattern | Cybersecurity example |
| --- | --- |
| “Someone used AI to…” | “Someone used AI to clone a helpdesk voice and reset an employee password” |
| “This changes…” | “This changes how phishing simulations need to be built” |
| “The scary part is not…” | “The scary part is not malware. It is AI-written trust.” |
| “Most teams missed…” | “Most teams missed the real risk in AI browser agents” |
| “Stop doing…” | “Stop pasting production logs into chatbots without a redaction layer” |
| “X examples…” | “9 ways AI agents can leak company data without ‘getting hacked’” |

## 5. Visual Style and Production

For this exact style, generate or source a dramatic image first, then apply text in a design template. Fully AI-generated text inside images is still risky for precise headlines, so use AI for the scene and use Canva, Figma, Photoshop, or a carousel tool for the final typography. That said, direct text-bearing image generation can work for concept drafts when exact text is not critical.

The recommended Instagram working size is **1080 × 1350 px**. Keep the main subject in the center and avoid putting critical text at the extreme edges because grid previews may crop differently.[1] [2]

| Layer | Recommendation |
| --- | --- |
| Canvas | 1080 × 1350 px portrait, consistent across all slides |
| Background | Cinematic cybersecurity image, high contrast, dark edges |
| Text band | Bottom 30–40% black gradient or solid black rectangle |
| Headline | Bold condensed sans-serif, all caps, white |
| Accent | One color: neon green, cyan, red, or electric blue |
| CTA | Small “SWIPE FOR MORE” under the headline |
| Brand mark | Tiny handle/logo centered above or below headline |
| Safe area | Keep important text and faces away from outer edges |

For tools, use a stack rather than one “magic” tool. aiCarousels can generate and export carousels from topics, text, URLs, PDFs, and YouTube content, and it includes templates and AI writing support.[3] Canva is useful for reusable branded templates. Figma is better if you want precise control and scalable systems. Midjourney, ChatGPT image generation, Ideogram, Recraft, or Leonardo can produce the cinematic base images. Buffer can help schedule and preview Instagram content, and its guide notes carousel sizing behavior and profile grid considerations.[2]

| Need | Suggested tools | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Research | Feedly, Google Alerts, Hacker News, Reddit, vendor blogs, arXiv, CVE feeds | Use primary sources when possible |
| Script writing | ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini | Use AI for drafts, not final fact-checking |
| Visual generation | Midjourney, ChatGPT image, Ideogram, Recraft, Leonardo | Generate backgrounds without exact long text |
| Layout | Canva, Figma, Photoshop, aiCarousels | Build reusable templates |
| Scheduling | Buffer, Later, Metricool, native Instagram tools | Preview grid and captions before posting |
| Trend tracking | X, LinkedIn, GitHub Trending, security newsletters | Build a daily swipe file |

## 6. Image Prompt Templates

For AI cybersecurity visuals, prompts should describe **scene, subject, lighting, mood, composition, and empty text area**. Avoid asking the image model to render the full final headline unless you are prepared to regenerate or manually correct it.

### Cover Image Prompt

```text
Create a cinematic vertical social media cover image for an AI cybersecurity carousel.
Subject: a security analyst in a dark SOC room watching an AI agent map exposed cloud assets across multiple monitors.
Composition: portrait 4:5, dramatic central subject, strong empty dark lower-third area for headline text, shallow depth of field, high contrast.
Style: realistic editorial tech photography, cyberpunk blue and green lighting, premium magazine cover feel, sharp details, no visible brand logos.
Text/content to render: no text.
Constraints: leave the bottom 35% clean and dark for typography, avoid distorted hands, avoid gibberish UI text, avoid copyrighted logos.
```

### Inner Slide Prompt

```text
Create a realistic cybersecurity explainer visual for an Instagram carousel.
Subject: a phishing email being generated from public social media profile data, shown abstractly as data streams moving into an AI model and then into an inbox.
Composition: portrait 4:5, clean focal point, dark background, space at top for a short slide title.
Style: cinematic, realistic, high-contrast, blue/green security color palette, subtle warning tone.
Text/content to render: no text.
Constraints: no real company logos, no readable personal information, no gore, no illegal instructions.
```

## 7. Caption Formula

The caption in the screenshot follows a simple structure: short headline, one-sentence event summary, two or three paragraphs explaining why it matters, a question, and a follow CTA. For cybersecurity, add a stronger fact-checking habit and avoid implying that a demo is already a widespread attack unless you know it is.

| Caption section | Purpose | Template |
| --- | --- | --- |
| First line | Restates the hook | “AI phishing just got harder to detect.” |
| Context | Explains what happened | “Attackers can now generate personalized pretexts using public employee data.” |
| Why it matters | Connects to risk | “The danger is not perfect malware. It is scalable trust abuse.” |
| Defender angle | Adds value | “Teams should update training, mailbox rules, and OAuth review processes.” |
| Comment question | Drives replies | “Would your team catch an email written around your actual LinkedIn posts?” |
| CTA | Converts viewers | “Follow for practical AI security breakdowns.” |

### Example Caption

```text
AI phishing just got harder to detect.

The old warning signs were bad grammar, weird links, and generic urgency. AI changes the equation because attackers can now generate messages that match a target’s role, company, recent posts, and internal-sounding context.

The risk is not that AI invented phishing. It is that AI makes personalization cheap enough to use at scale.

For defenders, this means awareness training has to evolve. Employees need to learn how to verify context, not just spot typos. Security teams should also review OAuth app approvals, mailbox rules, and suspicious login patterns.

Would your team catch an email written around your actual LinkedIn activity?

Follow for AI security breakdowns without the fake panic.

#AI #Cybersecurity #InfoSec #SocialEngineering #Phishing #AIsecurity
```

## 9. A Weekly Production Workflow

The repeatable system matters more than any single post. You want to batch the work so you are not reinventing the process every day.

| Day | Task | Output |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Monday | Collect stories from newsletters, X, GitHub, vendor blogs, and research feeds | 15–25 raw ideas |
| Tuesday | Score ideas by novelty, relevance, proof, and visual potential | 5 selected posts |
| Wednesday | Write carousel scripts and captions | 5 drafts |
| Thursday | Generate visuals and assemble slides | 5 finished carousels |
| Friday | Review facts, schedule posts, prepare alt captions | Scheduled queue |
| Daily | Check comments and save high-performing hooks | Feedback loop |

Use a simple scoring system before producing anything. Score each idea from 1–5 on **technical credibility**, **audience relevance**, **visual drama**, **novelty**, and **defender usefulness**. If an idea is dramatic but technically weak, either reframe it as a myth-bust or skip it.

## 10. Quality Control Checklist

This niche punishes sloppy claims. A post that gets attention for the wrong reason can damage trust with the exact audience you want: security practitioners.

| Check | Pass condition |
| --- | --- |
| Source quality | At least one primary or reputable source supports the claim |
| Claim wording | The cover does not exaggerate beyond the evidence |
| Technical accuracy | Attack paths and mitigations are not misleading |
| Safety | The post does not provide step-by-step abuse instructions |
| Visual readability | Headline is readable on a phone at thumbnail size |
| Brand consistency | Fonts, colors, CTA, and layout match previous posts |
| CTA | Caption asks a specific question rather than “thoughts?” |

## 11. The Best Starting Stack

If you want the fastest path without overengineering it, start with **ChatGPT or Claude for scripts**, **Midjourney or ChatGPT image generation for backgrounds**, **Canva for templates**, and **Buffer or native Instagram scheduling**. If you want more control and a future content machine, use **Figma templates** and keep a Notion/Airtable database of ideas, sources, hooks, captions, and performance.

| Maturity level | Stack |
| --- | --- |
| Beginner | ChatGPT + Canva + Instagram native posting |
| Faster creator | ChatGPT/Claude + Midjourney/Ideogram + Canva + Buffer |
| More professional | Research database + Figma template system + AI image generator + scheduled publishing |
| Agency-level | Airtable/Notion pipeline + reusable templates + automated drafts + manual fact-check gate + analytics review |

## 12. Practical Recommendation

Do not try to fully automate this at first. The highest-leverage move is to create **10 manually produced posts** using one template and measure which hook types work. After that, automate the boring parts: idea collection, first-draft captions, slide-outline generation, and scheduling. Keep manual control over technical claims, final headlines, and visual approval.

Your differentiator should be: **viral packaging, practitioner-grade judgment**. The stics.ai style proves the packaging works. The opportunity is to make an AI-cybersecurity version that people in security would actually respect.
